## What Are the SEC Cybersecurity Disclosure Rules?

On July 26, 2023, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) adopted final rules requiring public companies to disclose material cybersecurity incidents and provide annual disclosures about their cybersecurity risk management, strategy, and governance. These rules, codified in the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, represent the most significant federal cybersecurity disclosure mandate for public companies in United States history.

The rules address two fundamental information gaps that the SEC identified in the public markets. First, investors lacked timely, consistent information about material cybersecurity incidents affecting the companies they invest in. Second, investors had no standardized way to evaluate a company's cybersecurity risk management practices and board-level governance. The SEC determined that cybersecurity risks are among the most significant risks facing public companies, and that existing voluntary disclosure practices were insufficient and inconsistent.

The rules went into effect on September 5, 2023, with compliance dates phased based on filer status. All registrants, regardless of size, must now comply with both the incident disclosure (Form 8-K Item 1.05) and annual governance disclosure (Form 10-K Item 1C) requirements.

The SEC's cybersecurity rules do not prescribe specific cybersecurity frameworks or technical controls. Instead, they require disclosure of the company's approach to cybersecurity risk management, including whether and how the company uses any cybersecurity frameworks (such as NIST Cybersecurity Framework or NIST SP 800-53). This framework-agnostic approach gives companies flexibility while ensuring investors receive consistent, comparable information.

## Regulatory Background and Timeline

**March 2022.** SEC proposed cybersecurity disclosure rules for public companies.

**July 26, 2023.** SEC adopted final cybersecurity disclosure rules (Release No. 33-11216).

**September 5, 2023.** Rules became effective.

**December 18, 2023.** Form 8-K Item 1.05 incident disclosure requirements took effect for all registrants except smaller reporting companies.

**June 15, 2024.** Form 8-K Item 1.05 requirements took effect for smaller reporting companies.

**Fiscal years beginning on or after December 15, 2023.** Form 10-K Item 1C annual disclosure requirements apply.

The final rules are published in the Federal Register and codified in SEC regulations. The full adopting release is available at [https://www.sec.gov/rules/final/2023/33-11216.pdf](https://www.sec.gov/rules/final/2023/33-11216.pdf).

## Who Must Comply?

The SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules apply to:

**Domestic Registrants.** All U.S. public companies that file periodic reports with the SEC under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 must comply. This includes companies listed on NYSE, NASDAQ, and other U.S. exchanges.

**Foreign Private Issuers.** Foreign private issuers must provide comparable disclosures on Form 6-K (incident reporting) and Form 20-F (annual governance reporting).

**Smaller Reporting Companies (SRCs).** SRCs are subject to the same rules but received an extended compliance timeline for Form 8-K Item 1.05 requirements (180-day deferral).

**Emerging Growth Companies.** Emerging growth companies are fully subject to the rules with no exemptions.

**Note for Private Companies.** While private companies are not directly subject to SEC reporting requirements, the rules have significant indirect effects. Public company customers, investors, and acquirers increasingly expect private companies to demonstrate cybersecurity maturity consistent with these standards. Private companies considering an IPO should establish SEC-aligned cybersecurity governance well in advance.

## Form 8-K Item 1.05: Material Incident Disclosure

The most operationally impactful requirement is the mandatory disclosure of material cybersecurity incidents on Form 8-K within four business days of determining that an incident is material.

### What Triggers the Disclosure Obligation

The obligation is triggered not by the discovery of a cybersecurity incident, but by the determination that the incident is material. This is a critical distinction. The company must:

1. Detect and investigate the cybersecurity incident.
2. Determine whether the incident is material.
3. File Form 8-K within four business days of the materiality determination.

### Required Disclosures

Form 8-K Item 1.05 requires disclosure of:

- The material aspects of the nature, scope, and timing of the incident
- The material impact or reasonably likely material impact on the registrant, including its financial condition and results of operations

### What NOT to Disclose

The SEC explicitly stated that companies should NOT disclose:

- Specific technical information about the incident response
- Details about planned remediation steps
- Information about the company's cybersecurity defenses
- Vulnerability details that could be exploited by threat actors
- Any information that could impede law enforcement investigation

### National Security Delay

The Attorney General may grant a delay in filing if disclosure would pose a substantial risk to national security or public safety. To request a delay:

1. Contact the FBI to request a determination.
2. The FBI consults with the Attorney General.
3. If granted, the delay may be up to 30 business days, with possible extensions up to 60 business days.
4. In extraordinary circumstances, an additional delay of up to 60 business days may be granted through an SEC exemptive order.

### Amendments

Companies must amend their Form 8-K disclosure if material information about a previously reported incident changes or becomes available. This is done through a Form 8-K/A filing.

## Form 10-K Item 1C: Annual Cybersecurity Governance

Form 10-K Item 1C requires annual disclosure of the company's cybersecurity risk management, strategy, and governance. This is a recurring annual obligation, not triggered by any specific event.

### Risk Management and Strategy (Item 106(b))

Companies must describe their processes for assessing, identifying, and managing material risks from cybersecurity threats, including:

- Whether and how the processes have been integrated into the overall risk management system
- Whether the company engages assessors, consultants, auditors, or other third parties
- Whether the company has processes to oversee and identify material risks from use of third-party service providers
- Whether and how the company considers previous cybersecurity incidents in its risk assessment
- How the cybersecurity processes inform the company's overall business strategy, financial planning, and capital allocation

### Governance (Item 106(c))

Companies must describe:

**Board Oversight:**
- The board's role in overseeing cybersecurity risks
- Whether any board committee is responsible for cybersecurity oversight, and if so, which committee
- How the board or committee is informed about cybersecurity risks

**Management's Role:**
- Management's role in assessing and managing material cybersecurity risks
- The relevant expertise of management responsible for cybersecurity
- How management is informed about and monitors the prevention, detection, mitigation, and remediation of cybersecurity incidents
- Whether and how management reports cybersecurity information to the board

## Materiality Determination Framework

The concept of "materiality" is central to the SEC cybersecurity rules. A cybersecurity incident is material if there is a substantial likelihood that a reasonable investor would consider the information important in making an investment decision.

### Quantitative Factors

- Direct financial losses (ransom payments, system restoration costs)
- Business interruption costs and lost revenue
- Legal liability and litigation costs
- Regulatory fines and penalties
- Customer compensation and credit monitoring costs
- Insurance deductibles and premium increases
- Impact on earnings per share, revenue, or operating margin

### Qualitative Factors

- Reputational harm and brand damage
- Loss of competitive advantage (trade secret theft, IP exfiltration)
- Impact on customer trust and retention
- Regulatory consequences beyond financial penalties
- Loss of business partnerships or contracts
- Impact on ongoing business operations
- Harm to individuals (employees, customers, patients)

### Aggregation

The SEC noted that a series of related incidents may be individually immaterial but material in the aggregate. Companies must assess materiality not only on an incident-by-incident basis but also considering the cumulative impact of related incidents.

### Timing of Determination

Companies must make materiality determinations "without unreasonable delay." The SEC declined to define a specific timeframe but stated that companies should not delay investigation or materiality determination to avoid the disclosure obligation. The four-business-day clock starts when the materiality determination is made, not when the incident is discovered.

### Documentation

Companies should document their materiality determination process, including:

- The factors considered
- The information available at the time
- The individuals involved in the determination
- The rationale for the conclusion
- The date of the determination

This documentation is critical for demonstrating compliance and defending against potential SEC enforcement actions.

## Relationship to NIST SP 800-53 and Cybersecurity Frameworks

While the SEC rules do not mandate any specific cybersecurity framework, they require companies to describe their cybersecurity risk management processes and whether they use any frameworks. This creates a strong incentive to adopt recognized frameworks:

**NIST SP 800-53** is the comprehensive catalog of over 1,000 security and privacy controls organized into 20 families. Companies that implement SP 800-53 controls can demonstrate a rigorous, well-documented cybersecurity program in their Item 1C disclosures.

**NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0** provides an outcome-based framework (Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover) that maps directly to the types of risk management activities the SEC requires companies to disclose. CSF 2.0 maps to NIST SP 800-53 controls.

**ISO 27001** provides a certifiable information security management system (ISMS) framework that crosswalks to NIST SP 800-53.

**SOC 2** provides third-party attestation of security controls based on Trust Services Criteria, which crosswalk to NIST SP 800-53.

The key connection: Companies that adopt frameworks built on NIST SP 800-53 can demonstrate, in their annual Form 10-K disclosure, that their cybersecurity program is aligned with the federal government's gold standard for security controls. This strengthens investor confidence and reduces litigation risk.

Companies should also ensure their incident response capabilities align with NIST SP 800-61 (Computer Security Incident Handling Guide), as robust incident response processes directly support the ability to detect incidents, determine materiality, and file within the four-business-day window.

- [ ] Review and update cybersecurity disclosures annually

## Common Compliance Gaps

Based on 23+ years of cybersecurity experience and analysis of early SEC filings, these are the most frequently encountered compliance gaps:

1. **No Materiality Framework.** Companies lack a documented, repeatable process for determining whether a cybersecurity incident is material. Without this, the four-business-day clock becomes ambiguous and legal exposure increases.

2. **Disconnected Teams.** Cybersecurity incident response teams operate independently from legal, finance, and investor relations, creating bottlenecks when rapid materiality determinations and public disclosures are required.

3. **Boilerplate Annual Disclosures.** Item 1C disclosures that use generic, boilerplate language instead of providing specific, substantive information about the company's cybersecurity program. The SEC has signaled that vague disclosures will invite scrutiny.

4. **No Board Expertise.** Boards that lack members with cybersecurity knowledge or experience, making it difficult to demonstrate meaningful board oversight as required by Item 106(c).

5. **Missing Third-Party Risk Processes.** Companies that cannot describe how they oversee cybersecurity risks from third-party service providers, a specific requirement in Item 106(b).

6. **Inadequate Incident Detection.** Companies without robust detection capabilities may not discover incidents promptly, creating risk that the SEC views the delay between incident occurrence and discovery as "unreasonable."

7. **No Tabletop Exercises.** Companies that have never rehearsed the end-to-end process from incident detection through materiality determination to 8-K filing. The four-business-day window is extremely tight for companies encountering this process for the first time.

8. **Aggregation Blindness.** Companies that assess each incident in isolation without considering whether related incidents, individually immaterial, may be material in aggregate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the four-business-day rule?**
A: When a public company determines that a cybersecurity incident is material, it must file a Form 8-K Item 1.05 disclosure within four business days of that determination. The clock starts when the materiality determination is made, not when the incident is discovered. Weekends, federal holidays, and any day the SEC is closed do not count as business days.

**Q: What happens if we miss the four-business-day deadline?**
A: Failure to file a timely Form 8-K may result in SEC enforcement action, including fines, cease-and-desist orders, and potential personal liability for officers who certified the company's disclosure controls. Additionally, late filing can trigger private securities litigation from shareholders.

**Q: Do we have to disclose the technical details of the incident?**
A: No. The SEC explicitly stated that companies should not disclose specific technical details about their cybersecurity defenses, planned remediation, or vulnerability information that could be exploited. The disclosure should focus on the material aspects of the nature, scope, timing, and impact of the incident.

**Q: Can we delay disclosure for national security reasons?**
A: Yes. The Attorney General may grant a delay if disclosure would pose a substantial risk to national security or public safety. The initial delay may be up to 30 business days, with possible extensions. Companies should contact the FBI to initiate this process.

**Q: What counts as a "cybersecurity incident" under the rules?**
A: The SEC defines a cybersecurity incident as "an unauthorized occurrence, or a series of related unauthorized occurrences, on or conducted through a registrant's information systems that jeopardizes the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of a registrant's information systems or any information residing therein." This is a broad definition that includes ransomware, data breaches, denial-of-service attacks, and other cyberattacks.

**Q: Do the rules apply to incidents at third-party vendors?**
A: Yes. If a cybersecurity incident at a third-party service provider materially affects the registrant, the registrant must disclose it. The SEC's definition covers incidents on the registrant's information systems and information residing therein, which can include systems operated by third parties.

**Q: What should we include in our 10-K Item 1C disclosure?**
A: Item 1C requires disclosure of cybersecurity risk management and strategy (processes for assessing, identifying, and managing material cybersecurity risks, use of third parties, integration with overall risk management) and governance (board oversight, management's role and expertise, reporting processes). The disclosure should be specific and substantive, not boilerplate.

**Q: How do we determine if an incident is "material"?**
A: An incident is material if there is a substantial likelihood that a reasonable investor would consider the information important in making an investment decision. Consider both quantitative factors (financial impact, business interruption costs) and qualitative factors (reputational harm, regulatory consequences, competitive impact). The determination should consider the totality of circumstances, including potential aggregation with related incidents.

**Q: Are private companies affected by these rules?**
A: Private companies are not directly subject to SEC reporting requirements. However, private companies that serve as vendors to public companies should expect increased cybersecurity due diligence from their public company customers. Private companies preparing for an IPO should establish SEC-aligned cybersecurity governance before going public.

**Q: Do we need a specific cybersecurity framework?**
A: The SEC rules do not mandate any specific framework. However, companies must describe their risk management processes and whether they use any frameworks. Adopting a recognized framework such as NIST CSF 2.0 or NIST SP 800-53 provides a structured, defensible approach to cybersecurity and strengthens annual disclosures.
